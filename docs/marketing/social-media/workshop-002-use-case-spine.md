## Purpose

Turn the first audience wedge into two concrete, repeatable social stories.

The guiding product promise remains:

> Handwrite clean charts at paper speed, then transpose, copy, export, store, and edit them like structured iPad charts.

The story is not "messy paper becomes clean." The story is "paper-speed chart writing without paper-chart limitations."

## Selected Use Cases

### 1. Full Band / Horn Section

Audience:
- Bandleaders, MDs, arrangers-in-practice, gigging musicians responsible for getting charts to a band.

Situation:
- A bandleader needs one practical chord chart for the rhythm section to become usable chart versions for multiple players.
- The workflow should feel like rehearsal prep, not engraving.

Core proof:
- Write one clean chart quickly with Apple Pencil.
- Duplicate or create a chart variant.
- Use transpose / Concert-Bb-Eb views for player needs.
- Export/share PDFs.
- Keep the chart stored for future edits.

Hook territory:
- "Write one chart. Transpose it for the band. Send the PDFs."
- "One chart for rehearsal. Versions for the players."
- "Paper-speed chart writing, but the chart stays reusable."

Claims to avoid:
- Do not imply full horn-section notation parts.
- Do not imply automatic arrangement generation.
- Do not imply melody/staff notation entry.
- Do not imply the app is cleaning up a scanned or messy paper chart.

Best assets:
- TikTok/Reel: quick workflow proof.
- Instagram carousel: five-screen workflow breakdown.
- Apple custom product page: bandleader angle.
- Website section: "For bandleaders."

### 2. Wedding Gig / Key Changes

Audience:
- Wedding/corporate/event gig musicians, rhythm section players, bandleaders, and players preparing large song lists.

Situation:
- A singer or client wants songs in different keys.
- The musician needs quick iReal Pro-style chord charts that can be transposed on the fly.

Core proof:
- Create a quick chord chart from scratch.
- Set or change key / transpose chord symbols.
- Export or share the chart.
- Store it for later setlist reuse or editing.

Hook territory:
- "POV: the singer moved three songs and the band still needs charts."
- "Quick chord charts for new keys, without rewriting the page."
- "Gig prep when the keys keep changing."

Claims to avoid:
- Do not imply iChart imports songs or knows copyrighted changes.
- Do not show recognizable copyrighted charts.
- Do not imply playback/backing tracks.
- Do not imply full notation.

Best assets:
- TikTok/Reel: fast scenario.
- Instagram Story poll: "How often do singers change keys last minute?"
- Facebook group post: workflow question, only where allowed.
- Apple custom product page: gigging musician angle.

## Product-Truth Boundaries

Allowed claims:
- iPad-first chart writing.
- Apple Pencil input.
- Structured chord chart editing.
- Transpose chord symbols and use Concert/Bb/Eb views.
- Duplicate chart variants.
- Export/share PDFs.
- Store charts for later edits.

Careful wording:
- "Chart versions for Bb/Eb players" is safer than "horn parts."
- "Quick iReal Pro-style chord charts" is a workflow analogy, not an import/playback claim.
- "Handwrite clean charts" is safer than "clean up handwritten charts."
- "Transpose without rewriting" is stronger and more accurate than "auto-create every part."

Avoid:
- Full notation engraving.
- Melody or pitched-note entry.
- Playback/backing tracks.
- Automatic paper-chart cleanup.
- Automatic arranging.
- Importing copyrighted songs or setlists.
